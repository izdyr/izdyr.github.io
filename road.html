M 01
Foundation
Learn + Plan
You start from zero. This month is pure learning — no building yet. Rushing into hardware without knowing CAD is the #1 mistake beginners make.
Learn Fusion 360 — complete a beginner course (YouTube: Product Design Online)
Watch Arduino crash course (freeCodeCamp full tutorial)
Buy your tools: 3D printer (Bambu Lab A1 Mini ~$300), Arduino Mega, basic servo kit
Study the concept art sketches — measure proportions, decide final scale (life-size vs 60%)
Print 3–5 small test parts to understand your printer's tolerances
Month End → Full CAD plan of skeleton. Printer calibrated. Arduino blinking LEDs.
M 02
Skeleton
Mechanical
Design and print the internal rigid skeleton. This is the spine, ribcage frame, shoulder mounts, hip joint, and leg supports. It carries all the weight.
Model torso frame in Fusion 360 — ribcage structure with servo mounting points
Print and assemble torso skeleton — use M3/M4 bolts, not glue
Design telescoping arm structure (3 nested sections, spring-loaded or servo-driven)
Print arm segments — test range of motion manually first
Build hip/pelvis mount that keeps low center of gravity (reference your sketches)
Month End → Full printable skeleton assembled and standing on its own.
M 03
Electronics
Arduino + Servos
Wire the skeleton to life. Servo motors in shoulders, elbows, and head. Power distribution. This is where Baymax starts actually moving.
Mount MG996R servos at shoulder joints (strong enough to hold arm weight)
Wire Arduino Mega → PCA9685 servo driver board (controls 16 servos at once)
Program basic arm raise/lower sequence — test range, avoid stripping gears
Add neck servo for head turning (±45 degrees)
Build power system: 12V LiPo battery + buck converters for 5V/6V servo rails
Add LED strip inside chest for glow effect
Month End → Skeleton moves arms and head. Lights on. Powered by battery.
M 04
Head + Eyes
Character
The head is what makes it Baymax. The oval eyes, the rounded dome, the small nose. Get this right and people will instantly recognize it.
Model head dome in Fusion 360 — smooth hemisphere, exact proportions from sketches
Design oval eye housings with diffused acrylic lens covers
Wire NeoPixel LED matrices behind each eye — controllable expressions
Add Raspberry Pi inside head for voice + expression control (swap from Arduino)
Install small speaker + amp board inside chest cavity
Program voice lines: "I am Baymax, your personal healthcare companion"
Month End → Head complete with glowing eyes, speaks Baymax lines, neck turns.
M 05
Exterior Skin
Aesthetic
The inflatable vinyl look. You'll fake it with EVA foam panels shaped over the skeleton — painted white, smooth seams. This is where it goes from robot to Baymax.
Buy 10mm EVA foam sheets — heat-form them over printed skeleton guides
Cut body panel segments: chest, abdomen, upper arms, forearms, hands
Use contact cement + seam tape for clean joints between panels
Apply Plasti-Dip base coat → sand smooth → white spray paint
Attach panels to skeleton with hidden magnets (allows disassembly for repairs)
Make hands — foam fingers with slight curve, not fully articulated
Month End → Full white exterior. Looks like Baymax. Unmistakably him.
M 06
Integration
Final Boss
Bring everything together. Voice control, automated sequences, polish. This month is also for fixing everything that broke in months 2–5.
Add wake-word detection (Raspberry Pi + Vosk offline speech recognition)
Program interaction sequences: greet → scan → respond to simple commands
Add ultrasonic distance sensor in chest — "scans" people who approach
Fix all broken joints, reprinted parts, bad wiring from earlier months
Final paint touch-up, seam refinement, clean cable management
Film the reveal video
Month End → Baymax is alive. Voice-responsive, arm-moving, eye-glowing. Done.
